THE SCIENCE

The aim is a short behavioural read that survives contact with an academic partner and a sceptical CFO. The wrapper is ours. The science underneath is public and validated, and we say so.

What it is

Each experiment is an adapted Information Sampling Task. In the original, a participant uncovers tiles to gather evidence about an uncertain outcome and decides when to commit. The number of pieces sampled before deciding indexes reflection impulsivity, the tendency to act on too little or to over-gather. We skin it as a leadership decision under a visible cost and clock. EBC is our label for the sampling read.

Clark and colleagues, 2006, Information Sampling Task, part of the CANTAB battery. Reflection impulsivity. Known caveat: the task can over-estimate impulsivity if the cost structure is misread, Hauser and colleagues, 2017, so we keep the economics simple and run a comprehension check.

The ancestor we deliberately did not use

The closest relative is the beads task, the draws-to-decision measure of jumping to conclusions. We avoid it because it is clinically loaded (delusion research) and confounded by poor task comprehension. We borrow the logic, not the task.

McLean, Mattiske and Balzan, 2015, meta-analysis of the beads task and delusional ideation.

The simple-versus-complex twist

The cheap headline tile is the fluency lure: information that reads easily feels truer and safer, so people grab the clean summary and commit. We build the lure on cost and content, a cheap, confident, low-evidence tile, not on hard-to-read fonts. The disfluent-font-improves-reasoning effect failed to replicate at scale, so we do not rely on it.

Alter and Oppenheimer, 2009, processing fluency. Disfluency non-replication: Meyer and colleagues, 2015.

The optimum, and why it is honest

For each board we compute the sampling needed to reach a confident posterior, benchmarking against an 85% confidence target (band 80 to 90%), given the signal reliability of the board. It says gather until you are reasonably sure, no more. Committing on zero evidence is a coin flip by construction. The optimal band shown on the dial is computed live from the board, and is labelled illustrative until we have real cohort data. We never show invented cohort figures.

The say-do gap

We ask what you claim you do, then measure what you do. The discrepancy catches the leader who says they gather evidence but commits on the headline. This is the new, ownable read, and it is exactly the people who would rather look decisive, caught in the act.

The leaderboard rule

The score rewards the construct: the right call, sampling near the optimum, and resisting the cheap headline. Never speed, never raw points, or the game becomes a snapping machine that trains the pathology we treat.

What we may and may not claim

We own the name, the scenarios, the scoring and the composite. We do not own the Information Sampling Task or optimal stopping; those are public. EBC is not a validated metric until we validate it on our own cohorts. The defensible line is: a behavioural measure of decision behaviour in a simulated scenario.

A standing irony, kept in view

Fluent, confident output, including from an AI, is itself the fluency lure this game is built to catch. Treat polish as a reason to check, not to trust.
